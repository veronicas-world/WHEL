## Medicine was built on the male body

Modern medicine built its foundational evidence on the male body and then prescribed the results to everyone. For most of the twentieth century women of childbearing potential were kept out of clinical trials on the rationale of protecting a possible pregnancy, and the United States did not require women in federally funded clinical research until the [NIH Revitalization Act of 1993](https://orwh.od.nih.gov/toolkit/recruitment/history), which means that the body of evidence underneath modern prescribing was assembled, for generations, almost entirely from men. The exclusion was never confined to the trials, because the same assumption shaped the science beneath them: in pharmacology research male animals still outnumber female animals by roughly five to one, and across biology only about [15 percent of studies include both sexes](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6877896/), so the basic understanding of how a drug behaves was largely worked out in male tissue long before it reached a woman.

The cost of that default is measurable rather than theoretical, because when doses are calibrated on bodies that are not theirs women end up overmedicated, and they experience adverse drug reactions [nearly twice as often as men](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32503637/). The pattern is not subtle once anyone looks for it: when the Government Accountability Office examined the prescription drugs pulled from the United States market in the years around the turn of the century, it found that [eight of the ten](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-01-286r) posed greater health risks to women than to men. Half the population has spent decades taking medicine designed around the other half, and the harm has been visible in the data the entire time.

## The treatments already exist

There is a fact about women's health that the research system has never treated as one, which is that for many of these conditions the drugs that help are already here, approved for something else, prescribed off label, and working in millions of cases that the evidence base simply never recorded. This is not the exception in women's health but the standard of care, and the examples are not obscure: premenstrual dysphoric disorder and the symptoms of menopause are both managed with antidepressants prescribed [off label](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8987152/), the insulin resistance behind [polycystic ovary syndrome](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/polycystic-ovary-syndrome) is managed with metformin, a drug approved for diabetes, and the GnRH antagonists first developed for prostate cancer became standard care for [endometriosis](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/endometriosis) pain. Women's medicine has run on repurposing for decades, and no one has yet turned that everyday practice into a discipline.

The most famous repurposed drug in the world shows where that practice leads when someone takes it seriously. Sildenafil was developed to treat angina, and when it underperformed at the heart and produced an effect its makers had not anticipated, Pfizer brought it to market in 1998 as Viagra ([Ghofrani et al., 2006](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7097805/)). It cured nothing, and yet by managing a condition that men simply live with it built one of the largest drug franchises in the history of the industry.

## Built for management

The reason the rest of the field overlooks these conditions is structural, and it begins with the question their engines were built to answer, because drug discovery is organized around the cure, around finding the single molecule that eliminates the disease. That is the right model for cancer and for rare genetic disease, and it is where the AI drug-discovery companies point their engines, since a cure for a fatal disease is the blockbuster, which is why Recursion, BenevolentAI, Healx, and Insilico all aim in that direction.

The conditions that define women's health do not resolve to a cure, because endometriosis, PCOS, PMDD, adenomyosis, perimenopause, and vulvodynia are not eliminated so much as lived with and managed over years, and while they are rarely fatal they are routinely life-debilitating, the kind of condition that quietly reshapes a career, a relationship, and a decade of a person's life without ever appearing on a mortality table. Management is therefore a different medical question with a different evidence base, because the useful question is not which molecule erases the disease but which existing drug makes the condition livable, at what dose, in which phase of the cycle, and for which person, and the answer to that question lives in the places a cure-hunting engine has no reason to look: in off-label prescribing patterns, in the small trial that reports daily function rather than remission, and in the patient communities where women record, month after month, what actually helped them get through the week.

That last source is real evidence and we treat it as such, because patient communities are where the practical knowledge of how to manage these conditions accumulates years ahead of the literature, so we read that signal, structure it, and test it against mechanistic and clinical evidence, without ever mistaking it for proof on its own and without ever discarding it as noise.

None of this describes a small or marginal market, because women are half the population and these conditions are common rather than rare: endometriosis affects roughly [one in ten](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/endometriosis) women of reproductive age, [PCOS](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/polycystic-ovary-syndrome) affects a comparable share, and every woman who lives long enough eventually reaches menopause. The work is undervalued for a narrower reason, which is that managing a chronic and non-fatal condition has never carried the prestige of curing a rare disease or beating cancer, so the attention and the capital flow toward the conditions that fit the cure model and away from the ones that do not, and the result is a large and proven market that almost no one is building for. The demand for making a life livable is not hypothetical, since Viagra went on to earn [billions of dollars a year](https://www.statista.com/statistics/264827/pfizers-worldwide-viagra-revenue-since-2003/) at its peak by managing a condition that kills no one.

A platform tuned to find cures reads all of this as static, and we read it as the data, because an engine built to find oncology blockbusters will reliably find oncology blockbusters while the drug that manages endometriosis for millions of women is exactly the kind of result it is not built to see, and that result is the one we are built to find.

## Why now

Two forces make this the moment to build, and both of them arrived recently. The first is that the economics and the momentum of drug development have moved decisively toward AI and toward repurposing, and the economics were always favorable, because a new drug costs well over a billion dollars and takes more than a decade while roughly nine in ten candidates fail, whereas a repurposed drug carries an established safety record, reaches patients sooner, and clears regulatory review at a far higher rate through pathways like [505(b)(2)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0022354923001508). The capital has now followed that logic at scale, since AI drug discovery spans more than 530 companies and AI biotech pulled in [5.6 billion dollars in a single year](https://intuitionlabs.ai/articles/ai-biotech-funding-trends), close to a third of all healthcare startup funding, while Isomorphic Labs alone raised 600 million dollars and signed discovery deals with Eli Lilly and Novartis worth up to 1.7 and 1.2 billion dollars; almost all of that money is aimed at novel molecules for the diseases that fit the cure model, which leaves the repurposing of approved drugs to manage female biology sitting almost entirely outside the field's attention.

The second force is that the same tools the discovery companies aim at novel chemistry can now be aimed at evidence instead, so that the scattered, unstructured, decades-deep record of how approved drugs behave in female bodies can finally be read at scale, and the literature, the trial registries, the adverse-event databases, and the patient communities can be read together for the first time. The signal was always there and the means to surface it has only just arrived, which is what makes this the moment that drug repurposing becomes the frontier of drug development and the moment that a platform dedicated to female biology becomes possible to build, and we intend to build it first.

## What Whel is

Whel is a drug-repurposing platform for female biology, which means that we find the approved drugs that already work for women's health conditions and prove it rigorously enough for a researcher or a clinician to act on, and we do that across three layers.

**The substrate.** A corrected knowledge graph that captures sex-specific pharmacokinetics, cyclical hormonal state, and the cross-condition mechanisms that general platforms miss because they were trained on male-default data, grounded in the standard biomedical ontologies, including MONDO, HPO, RxNorm, and ChEMBL, and extended with female-specific concepts that no existing ontology covers adequately.

**Retrieval and validation.** Provenance-preserving extraction tuned for biomedical literature, in which every claim ties to a verbatim source span, every synthesis is marked as a synthesis, and every contradiction in the underlying literature is shown rather than averaged away, which is both the discipline that the FDA's 21st Century Cures Act §3060 research-support exemption requires and the discipline a clinician needs in order to trust the output.

**Hypothesis from signal.** Patient-community signal, including off-label prescribing patterns, advocacy-organization registries, and structured patient reports, which enters as hypothesis generation and is validated downstream against mechanistic and clinical evidence, never equated with the results of a controlled trial and never discarded, because it is the input that surfaces the hypotheses worth checking.

The output of all three is a set of repurposing candidates that a researcher can act on, each one carrying its full evidence trail, and we begin where the need is sharpest and the signal is densest before expanding across the biology that medicine left unstudied, starting with conditions whose scale alone should have commanded attention long ago: [endometriosis](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/endometriosis) affects about 190 million women with diagnosis still delayed by seven to nine years on average, and [PCOS](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/polycystic-ovary-syndrome) is the most common endocrine disorder in women of reproductive age while as many as 70 percent of the women who have it remain undiagnosed.

The biology is real, the drugs are already in pharmacies, and the evidence is waiting to be read; the record was written for the wrong body, and we are correcting it.
